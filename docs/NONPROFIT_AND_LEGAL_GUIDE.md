## 1. Forming a 501(c)(3) Nonprofit

A 501(c)(3) designation means the IRS recognizes your organization as tax-exempt, and donations to you are tax-deductible for donors. Here is the step-by-step process.

### Step 1: Choose Your State and Incorporate

You need to form a nonprofit corporation at the state level before applying for federal tax-exempt status. Most people incorporate in the state where they operate.

**What to do:**

1. **Pick a name.** Confirm "The Fullest Project" (or your chosen legal name) is available in your state. Most Secretaries of State have an online business name search tool.
2. **File Articles of Incorporation.** This is a short document filed with your state's Secretary of State. It must include:
   - The organization's name and address
   - A statement of purpose (use IRS-approved language — something like: *"This corporation is organized exclusively for charitable and educational purposes within the meaning of Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code."*)
   - A dissolution clause (stating that if the org shuts down, remaining assets go to another 501(c)(3), not to individuals)
   - The name and address of a registered agent (someone who can receive legal mail — can be a founder)
   - Names of initial directors
3. **Pay the filing fee.** Typically **$50–$150** depending on the state.

**Timeline:** Filing usually takes 1–4 weeks. Some states offer expedited processing for an extra fee.

### Step 2: Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number)

An EIN is like a Social Security Number for your organization. You need it to open a bank account, file taxes, and apply for 501(c)(3) status.

**What to do:**

- Apply online at irs.gov — it is free and you get the number immediately.
- You will need a "responsible party" (one of the founders) with a valid SSN.

**Cost:** Free.

### Step 3: Draft Bylaws

Bylaws are the internal rulebook for how your organization operates. They are not filed with the state but are required by the IRS.

**Your bylaws should cover:**

- Organization name and purpose
- Membership (if any — many small nonprofits have no formal members)
- Board of directors: how many, how they are elected/appointed, term lengths, how vacancies are filled
- Officers: titles (President, Secretary, Treasurer at minimum), duties, how they are selected
- Meeting rules: how often the board meets, quorum requirements (the minimum number of board members needed to make decisions), how meetings can be called
- Conflict of interest policy (the IRS specifically looks for this)
- Amendment procedures (how bylaws can be changed)
- Fiscal year (most use January–December or July–June)

Many free bylaws templates exist online. The IRS provides a sample conflict of interest policy in the instructions for Form 1023.

### Step 4: Hold an Organizational Board Meeting

At your first official board meeting:

- Adopt the bylaws
- Elect officers
- Authorize opening a bank account
- Adopt a conflict of interest policy
- Keep written minutes — the IRS may ask for them

### Step 5: Board of Directors Requirements

**Minimum board size:** Most states require at least **1 director**, but the IRS strongly prefers **at least 3**. Best practice is 3–5 for a small startup.

**Key points:**

- Board members are unpaid volunteers (they can be reimbursed for expenses, but should not receive salaries as board members).
- Erin and Nicole can both serve on the board, but it is good practice to have at least one "independent" member (someone not related to the founders by family or business).
- Common roles: **President/Chair**, **Secretary**, **Treasurer**. In a small org, one person can hold multiple officer roles, but President and Secretary should ideally be different people.
- Board members have fiduciary duties — they must act in the organization's best interest, not their own.

### Step 6: Apply for 501(c)(3) Status with the IRS

This is the federal application for tax-exempt status.

**Form 1023 vs. Form 1023-EZ:**

| | Form 1023 (Full) | Form 1023-EZ (Streamlined) |
|---|---|---|
| **Eligibility** | Any organization | Must have gross receipts of $50,000/year or less for past 3 years AND total assets of $250,000 or less |
| **Length** | 28+ pages with attachments | About 3 pages (online only) |
| **Filing fee** | **$600** | **$275** |
| **Processing time** | 3–6 months (sometimes longer) | 2–4 weeks |
| **Recommended for** | Larger organizations | **Small startups like The Fullest Project** |

**Recommendation for The Fullest Project:** Use **Form 1023-EZ** if you qualify. You almost certainly do as a new organization. Check the IRS eligibility worksheet first (available in the 1023-EZ instructions).

**How to file:**

- Form 1023-EZ is filed online through pay.gov.
- You will need your EIN, Articles of Incorporation, and bylaws ready.
- Describe your activities clearly: operating a disability resource directory website, providing educational content, connecting people with disabilities to resources, and selling disability-positive merchandise.

### Step 7: State Tax Exemption

After receiving your federal 501(c)(3) determination letter:

- Apply for **state income tax exemption** (process varies by state — some grant it automatically, others require a separate application).
- Apply for **sales tax exemption** if your state offers it for nonprofits (this exempts purchases you make for the org, not necessarily sales you make).

### Step 8: Register for Charitable Solicitation

If you plan to fundraise (accept donations) from the public, **most states require registration** before you start soliciting.

- About **40 states** require this. Requirements vary widely.
- Typically involves filing a form and paying a fee ($0–$250 depending on the state).
- You generally only need to register in states where you actively solicit donations.
- If you have a "Donate" button on your website, some states consider that solicitation in their state.
- Services like **Harbor Compliance** or **File990** can help manage multi-state registration, but they charge fees.

**Tip for starting out:** At minimum, register in your home state. Expand to other states as your fundraising grows.

### Step 9: Annual Compliance

Once established, you must maintain your status every year:

| Requirement | Details | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| **IRS Form 990** | Annual information return. Small orgs (gross receipts of $50,000 or less) file the **990-N (e-Postcard)** — it is free and takes 5 minutes online. | 5 and a half months after fiscal year end |
| **State annual report** | Most states require an annual or biennial report with a small fee ($0–$50). | Varies by state |
| **Charitable solicitation renewal** | If registered, renewals are usually annual. | Varies by state |
| **Board meetings** | Hold at least one board meeting per year and keep minutes. | Ongoing |

**Critical warning:** If you fail to file Form 990 for **three consecutive years**, the IRS **automatically revokes** your tax-exempt status.

### Total Estimated Startup Costs

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| State incorporation (Articles of Incorporation) | $50–$150 |
| EIN | Free |
| IRS Form 1023-EZ | $275 |
| Charitable solicitation registration (home state) | $0–$250 |
| **Total** | **$325–$675** |

Optional: Attorney review of documents ($500–$2,000, but free options exist — see Section 5).

## 2. Legal Considerations

### Liability Disclaimers for a Resource Directory

As a resource hub that lists providers, services, and organizations, The Fullest Project needs to protect itself from liability if a listed resource turns out to be problematic.

**Add these disclaimers to your website:**

1. **General Disclaimer:** *"The Fullest Project provides this directory for informational purposes only. Listing a resource does not constitute an endorsement, recommendation, or guarantee of the quality, safety, or suitability of any service, provider, or organization listed. Users should independently verify the credentials and suitability of any resource before engaging with it."*

2. **No Professional Advice Disclaimer:** *"The information on this website is not a substitute for professional medical, legal, financial, or therapeutic advice. Always consult qualified professionals for advice specific to your situation."*

3. **External Links Disclaimer:** *"This site contains links to third-party websites. The Fullest Project is not responsible for the content, accuracy, or practices of external sites."*

**Where to place these:**
- A dedicated "Disclaimer" page linked in the footer
- At the top or bottom of resource directory pages
- In your Terms of Service

### Privacy Policy

A privacy policy is **legally required** if you collect any personal information (names, emails, analytics data, etc.). This is especially important given you work with disability-related information, which is considered sensitive.

**Your privacy policy should cover:**

- What personal information you collect (names, emails, browsing data, cookies)
- How you collect it (forms, analytics tools, cookies)
- Why you collect it (newsletter, contact forms, improving the site)
- How you store and protect it
- Who you share it with (third-party services like analytics, email providers)
- How users can request deletion of their data
- Whether you collect information from children (see COPPA below)
- Contact information for privacy questions

**Key considerations for disability-related data:**

- **Minimize data collection.** Do not collect disability-specific information unless absolutely necessary.
- If users self-identify as having a disability (e.g., through a survey or account profile), treat that as **sensitive personal information** with extra protection.
- If you collect health-related data, be aware that some states have specific health data privacy laws (e.g., Washington's My Health My Data Act).
- The **ADA itself does not regulate data privacy**, but collecting and mishandling disability data could create legal and ethical risks.

**Tools to help:** Free privacy policy generators like Termly, TermsFeed, or FreePrivacyPolicy.com can give you a solid starting template. Have a lawyer review it if possible.

### ADA and WCAG Compliance

As a disability resource hub, your website should be a model of accessibility. This is both a legal and ethical imperative.

**Legal landscape:**

- **ADA Title III** has been interpreted by courts and the DOJ to apply to websites, especially those connected to organizations serving the public.
- The DOJ has issued guidance stating that websites should conform to **WCAG 2.1 Level AA** standards.
- Lawsuits against inaccessible websites are common and increasing (thousands filed per year).

**WCAG 2.1 Level AA — Key requirements:**

- **Perceivable:** Alt text on all images, captions on videos, sufficient color contrast (4.5:1 ratio for normal text), content readable when zoomed to 200%
- **Operable:** Full keyboard navigation, no keyboard traps, skip navigation links, no content that flashes more than 3 times per second, descriptive page titles
- **Understandable:** Clear language, consistent navigation, labels on form fields, error identification and suggestions
- **Robust:** Valid HTML, proper use of ARIA attributes, compatibility with screen readers

**Practical steps:**

1. Use semantic HTML (headings in order, proper landmarks, lists for lists)
2. Test with a screen reader (NVDA is free for Windows, VoiceOver is built into Mac/iOS)
3. Run automated checks with free tools: axe DevTools browser extension, WAVE, Lighthouse
4. Test keyboard navigation — can you use the entire site without a mouse?
5. Ensure sufficient color contrast — use a contrast checker tool
6. Add an accessibility statement page describing your commitment and how to report issues

### Terms of Service

A Terms of Service (ToS) page protects your organization by setting rules for using your website.

**Include:**

- Acceptable use policy (no scraping, no harassment, no misuse)
- Intellectual property notice (your content is yours)
- Disclaimer of warranties ("as-is" basis)
- Limitation of liability
- User-generated content policy (if you allow reviews or comments)
- Governing law and dispute resolution
- Right to modify terms
- Contact information

### Intellectual Property — Trademarking "The Fullest Project"

**Why trademark?** A federal trademark protects your name and logo nationwide and prevents others from using a confusingly similar name for similar services.

**Process:**

1. **Search first.** Use the USPTO's Trademark Electronic Search System (TESS) to check if "The Fullest Project" or similar names are already trademarked.
2. **File an application** with the USPTO through the Trademark Electronic Application System (TEAS).
3. **Costs:**
   - TEAS Plus filing: **$250 per class** (a "class" is a category of goods/services — you would likely file in Class 35 for nonprofit services and Class 41 for educational services)
   - TEAS Standard filing: **$350 per class**
   - Total for two classes: **$500–$700**
4. **Timeline:** 8–12 months from filing to registration if there are no issues.
5. **Maintenance:** You must file maintenance documents between the 5th and 6th year after registration, and then every 10 years.

**Alternative for now:** Even without a federal trademark, you have some "common law" trademark rights just by using the name in commerce. You can use the TM symbol (not the circled R) without registration.

**Recommendation:** Trademarking is worthwhile but not urgent. Prioritize it once the organization is established and has some revenue. Budget $500–$700 if doing it yourself, or $1,500–$3,000 with an attorney.

### Insurance

**Types of insurance to consider:**

| Type | What It Covers | Estimated Annual Cost | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Directors & Officers (D&O)** | Protects board members from personal liability for decisions made on behalf of the org | $500–$2,000/year | High — get this early |
| **General Liability** | Covers bodily injury, property damage, and related claims | $400–$1,500/year | Medium — important if hosting events |
| **Cyber Liability** | Covers data breaches and cyber incidents | $500–$2,000/year | Medium — especially if collecting personal data |
| **Professional Liability (E&O)** | Covers claims of negligence or harm from your services/advice | $500–$2,000/year | Low initially — more relevant as services expand |

**Recommendation:** Start with **D&O insurance** at minimum. Look into nonprofit insurance packages from providers like **Nonprofits Insurance Alliance (NIA)**, **The Hartford**, or **Hiscox**, which bundle multiple coverages at reduced rates.

### Volunteer vs. Employee Classifications

**This matters for legal and tax reasons.** Misclassifying workers can result in penalties.

- **Volunteers:** Can only be used by nonprofits (not for-profit businesses). Volunteers cannot be paid, though they can receive small non-cash benefits (t-shirts, meals). They cannot replace what would otherwise be a paid position at a for-profit company.
- **Employees:** Must receive at least minimum wage, you must withhold taxes, pay employer payroll taxes, provide workers' compensation insurance, etc.
- **Independent Contractors:** If you hire someone for a specific project (e.g., a web developer, graphic designer), they may be a contractor. The IRS uses a multi-factor test focusing on the degree of control you have over how, when, and where the work is done.

**For The Fullest Project starting out:** Erin and Nicole are likely uncompensated founders/board members (volunteers). If the org grows and starts paying people, consult a CPA about proper classification and payroll requirements.

### Data Protection and COPPA

**COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act)** applies if your website:

- Is directed at children under 13, OR
- Knowingly collects personal information from children under 13

**If The Fullest Project serves minors (or their families):**

- If you do **not** specifically target children under 13 and do not knowingly collect their data, COPPA likely does not apply. However, as a disability resource hub, families with children may use the site.
- **Safest approach:** Add a statement that the site is not directed at children under 13 and that you do not knowingly collect data from children under 13. Provide a mechanism for parents to request deletion of their child's data if inadvertently collected.
- If you **do** plan features specifically for children (e.g., a kids' section, youth accounts), you must comply with COPPA: get verifiable parental consent before collecting data, post a clear privacy policy, and more. This is complex — consult a lawyer if you go this route.

**State privacy laws to be aware of:**

- **California (CCPA/CPRA):** Applies to larger businesses, but nonprofits are currently exempt. Still good practice to follow its principles.
- Various other states have passed privacy laws, most of which currently exempt nonprofits, but the trend is toward broader coverage.

## 3. E-Commerce / Selling Products

Selling disability-positive merchandise is a great way to raise awareness and generate revenue. Here are your options.

### Print-on-Demand vs. Inventory

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Print-on-Demand (POD)** | No upfront cost, no inventory risk, no shipping hassles, wide product variety | Lower profit margins (roughly 20–40%), less control over quality, longer shipping times |
| **Holding Inventory** | Higher margins, faster shipping, full quality control | Upfront investment, storage needs, risk of unsold stock |

**Recommendation for starting out:** Use **print-on-demand**. It has zero upfront cost and zero risk. You can switch to inventory later for best-selling designs once you know what works.

### Platform Comparison

| Platform | Best For | Fees/Costs | POD Built-in? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bonfire** | Nonprofits, campaign-based sales | Free to set up, they handle everything, you set your markup | Yes — designed for this |
| **Spring (formerly Teespring)** | Simple merch stores | Free to set up, you set price above base cost | Yes |
| **Printful + Shopify** | Full-featured online store | Printful: free to set up; Shopify: $39/mo (nonprofit discount sometimes available) | Yes (Printful is the POD provider, Shopify is the storefront) |
| **Printful + Etsy** | Reaching existing shoppers | Printful: free; Etsy: $0.20/listing + 6.5% transaction fee | Yes |
| **Shopify alone** | Selling your own inventory | $39/mo + payment processing fees | No (but integrates with POD apps) |
| **Etsy alone** | Handmade/unique items, existing marketplace traffic | $0.20/listing + 6.5% transaction fee | No |
| **Fourthwall** | Creator/community merch stores | Free plan available, they handle fulfillment | Yes |

**Top recommendation for The Fullest Project:** Start with **Bonfire**. It is specifically popular with nonprofits and cause-based organizations. No upfront cost, no monthly fees, and they handle printing, shipping, and customer service. You just upload designs and share your store link.

**Second choice:** **Spring** or **Fourthwall** for a free standalone merch store with more product options.

**Future upgrade:** When revenue justifies it, move to **Shopify + Printful** for a fully branded, professional store on your own domain.

### UBIT — Unrelated Business Income Tax

Nonprofits can absolutely sell merchandise, but the IRS distinguishes between related and unrelated business income.

- **Related income** (not taxed): Merchandise that furthers your mission. Disability-positive apparel, educational materials, and awareness items from The Fullest Project would likely be considered **related** to your charitable/educational purpose.
- **Unrelated income** (taxed at corporate rates via Form 990-T): Selling items that have nothing to do with your mission.
- **Volunteer exception:** If substantially all the work is done by volunteers (unpaid), the income is exempt from UBIT regardless.

**For The Fullest Project:** Your disability-positive merchandise almost certainly qualifies as related to your mission AND is likely run by volunteers. You are very unlikely to owe UBIT. But keep good records of how merchandise relates to your mission.

### Payment Processing

| Provider | Transaction Fees | Nonprofit Discount? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stripe** | 2.9% + $0.30 | Yes — reduced rates for 501(c)(3)s upon request | Industry standard, works with almost everything |
| **PayPal** | 2.89% + $0.49 | Yes — 1.99% + $0.49 for confirmed nonprofits | Widely trusted by donors |
| **Square** | 2.6% + $0.10 (in person), 2.9% + $0.30 (online) | Not typically | Good if you do in-person events |

**Recommendation:** **Stripe** is the best overall choice for online sales and donations. Apply for their nonprofit discounted rate after getting your 501(c)(3) determination letter.

### Sales Tax Considerations

This is one of the more complex areas for online sellers.

**Key concepts:**

- **Nexus:** You owe sales tax in a state if you have a "nexus" (connection) there. Physical presence (office, warehouse, employees) creates nexus. Since the 2018 *South Dakota v. Wayfair* decision, **economic nexus** also applies — if your sales exceed a threshold in a state (commonly $100,000 in sales or 200 transactions), you may owe sales tax there.
- **Nonprofit exemption:** Having 501(c)(3) status exempts you from **federal income tax**, but it does **not** automatically exempt you from **state sales tax**. Each state has different rules. Some exempt nonprofits from collecting sales tax, many do not.
- **If using POD:** Platforms like Bonfire and Spring typically handle sales tax collection and remittance for you. This is a major advantage.

**Recommendation:** When starting out, use a platform (Bonfire, Spring) that handles sales tax for you. If you later run your own Shopify store, use Shopify's built-in tax calculation or a service like TaxJar or Avalara.

### Integrating a Shop with Your Static Site

If thefullestproject.org is a static site (HTML/CSS or a static site generator), you have several options to add shopping:

1. **Link to an external store** (simplest): Add a "Shop" link in your navigation that goes to your Bonfire, Spring, or Shopify store. The store lives on a separate URL.
2. **Embed widgets:** Many platforms offer embeddable buy buttons or product widgets you can paste into your HTML.
   - Shopify has a "Buy Button" that generates embed code for any website
   - Bonfire and Spring offer shareable product links and basic embeds
3. **Subdomain approach:** Set up shop.thefullestproject.org pointing to your Shopify store for a more branded experience.

**Recommendation:** Start by simply linking to a Bonfire store. It takes 30 minutes to set up and requires zero changes to your existing site beyond adding a navigation link.

### Fulfillment and Shipping

- **POD platforms** handle fulfillment and shipping entirely. You never touch a product.
- Typical POD shipping times: 5–10 business days (production + transit).
- Offer transparency on your store page about shipping times.
- If you later hold your own inventory, look into **ShipStation** or **Pirate Ship** for discounted shipping labels.

### Fiscal Sponsorship — An Alternative to Full 501(c)(3)

If you want to start fundraising **before** your 501(c)(3) is approved (or if you decide not to incorporate), fiscal sponsorship is an option.

**How it works:** An existing 501(c)(3) organization "sponsors" your project. Donations go through them (making them tax-deductible), and they pass the funds to you, minus an administrative fee (typically 5–10%).

**Popular fiscal sponsors:**

- **Fractured Atlas** — great for creative/arts projects (7% fee)
- **Social Good Fund** — broad focus (7% fee)
- **Open Collective Foundation** — tech-friendly, transparent finances (10% fee)
- **Third Sector New England / TSNE** — New England-based but serves nationally

**Pros:** Start fundraising immediately, no incorporation needed, no annual IRS filings on your end.

**Cons:** Less autonomy, ongoing fees, you do not own the 501(c)(3) status.

**Recommendation:** Fiscal sponsorship is a good **bridge strategy** — use it while your 501(c)(3) application is pending (which can take months for Form 1023 or weeks for 1023-EZ). If the 1023-EZ timeline works for you, you may not need fiscal sponsorship at all.

### Corporate Partnerships and Sponsorships

As a disability-focused organization, you may attract corporate sponsors interested in disability inclusion, accessibility, or social responsibility.

**Approaches:**

1. **Sponsorship packages:** Create tiers (e.g., "Gold Sponsor — $5,000 — logo on homepage for one year"). Offer logo placement, social media mentions, newsletter features.
2. **Cause marketing partnerships:** A company donates a percentage of sales of a product to your organization.
3. **In-kind sponsorships:** Companies donate goods or services (web hosting, design work, printing) instead of cash.
4. **Employee giving programs:** Many large companies match employee donations or have volunteer grant programs. Make sure you are registered on platforms like **Benevity** and **YourCause** so employees can find you.

**Companies known for disability inclusion partnerships:** Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, Procter & Gamble, Accenture, EY, Bank of America — check their corporate social responsibility pages for partnership applications.

**Tip:** Start small. Local businesses are often more accessible and more willing to support local/grassroots nonprofits.

You can get free or heavily discounted access to Microsoft 365, Adobe Creative Cloud, Slack, Zoom Pro, QuickBooks, Canva Pro, and dozens of other tools. This alone can save you thousands of dollars per year.
